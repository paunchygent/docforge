Student Debate Guide

Concise reference for prepping, speaking, and judging practice rounds.

Format & Timing

  • Teams: Affirmative versus Negative, three speakers each — six speeches total.
  • Order: Aff Opener → Neg Opener → Aff Builder → Neg Builder → Aff Closer → Neg Closer.
  • Length: Each speech lasts 3:00. Use 1:00 versions in drills to sharpen structure.
  • POIs: Optional. Maximum two per team per speech, ≤10 seconds. The speaker may accept or decline.

Speaker Conduct

  • Respect: Interrupt only for POIs. Stay polite even under pressure.
  • Timing: Finish when the signal sounds; a clean close sounds confident.
  • Delivery: Use a steady voice, eye contact, and grounded posture to boost credibility.

Addressing Audience & Opponents

  • Audience first: Frame arguments to the judges and room before turning to the opposing team.
  • Language: Use phrases such as “This team argues…” instead of personal names.
  • Signposting: Use connectors (firstly, however, therefore, to conclude) to highlight structure.
  • Clarity: Keep sentences short with one idea each to aid ESL listeners. This will also help you stay focused on your main points.

Speaker Roles

Opener

  • State the thesis in one sentence and define key terms if needed.
  • Deliver one SEAL point (Statement → Example → Argument [why it matters] → Link back to the motion).
  • Include at least two spoken connectors to guide listeners.

Builder

  • Add a fresh SEAL point with a new example that reinforces the team line.
  • Handle one priority rebuttal (respond to their main point) without taking focus from your main extension (your new argument).

Closer

  • Summarise the team’s stance, weighing your strongest points against theirs.
  • Deliver concise rebuttal and finish with the thesis line.

Building Every Speech

  • Use SEAL at least once; always state the Link back to the motion.
  • Choose examples you can explain in two lines rather than just naming sources.
  • Verbalise at least two connectors to signal transitions.
  • Body language (stance, hands, eye contact) should underline the argument, not distract.

Points of Information (POIs)

POIs are short questions (≤10 seconds) offered to the speaker by the opposing team. Their purpose is to challenge a point or force clarification.

As the Speaker:

  • You may accept or decline a POI. It is wise to accept at least one.
  • To accept, say "Yes, please" or simply gesture. To decline, wave a hand dismissively.
  • Answer the question concisely (in one sentence), then return immediately to your speech.

As the Asker:

  • Stand and say, "Point of information."
  • If accepted, ask your single, clear question.
  • Sit down as soon as you have finished asking.

Rebuttals: Prepared vs. Live

A rebuttal is a response to an argument from the opposing team. Your goal is to show why their point is wrong, irrelevant, or less important than yours. There are two types:

  • Prepared Rebuttals: These are arguments you prepare in advance for points you expect the other team to make (see the "Rebuttal sheet" in the checklist).
  • Live (Tailored) Rebuttals: These are direct responses to the specific arguments the other team actually makes during the debate. Winning debates requires good live rebuttals.

When to Rebut

  • Rebuttals are usually delivered at the start of a speech, before you introduce your own new arguments.
  • The Builder speakers should rebut the previous speaker before building their own case.
  • Closer speakers focus heavily on rebuttal to show why your side won.

How to Structure a Live Rebuttal (4 Steps)

  1. "They Said..." — Briefly and fairly state the argument you are responding to.
  2. "But We Disagree..." — State your direct counter-argument.
  3. "Because..." — Explain why you are right, using logic or an example.
  4. "Therefore..." — Explain the impact of your rebuttal on the debate (e.g., "...therefore, their point about cost is invalid and our plan remains the best option.").

Questions from Chair or Judges

  • Answer in two lines: give a clear claim, then a reason supporting it.
  • Keep tone calm and formal; treat questions as a chance to reinforce your case.

Audience & Judge Focus

  • Use the judge score card to track clarity, structure, and the evidence you actually understood.
  • Note signposting, rhetorical devices, and links back to the motion to see who stays on task.
  • Observe how teams handle POIs, rebuttals, and maintain poise and presence under time pressure.
  • Choose the side with the clearer, better-supported case — not the loudest delivery.

Quick Prep Checklist

  • Thesis line drafted and agreed.
  • Three SEAL points mapped to Opener, Builder, Closer roles.
  • Rebuttal sheet filled: “They will say…” / “Our reply…”
  • Two connectors you will actually say, highlighted.

Use this guide before, during, and after practice debates to keep structure tight and teams aligned.
