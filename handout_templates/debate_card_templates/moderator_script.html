Moderator Script — Team Debates

Scripts, transitions, and timing protocols for running debate rounds smoothly and professionally.

Opening Script

"Good afternoon. This round follows the six-speech format: three speakers per side. Openers have two minutes, Builders and Closers have three minutes each. During Builder speeches only, the opposing team may offer one 10-second Point of Information. After the closers, we will hold a five-minute POI stage with alternating questions. Judges will score clarity and argument strength, not personal opinion.

[Read debate topic introduction here — see section below]

The motion reads: [insert motion here]. We now begin."

Format & Timing

  • Order: Aff Opener → Neg Opener → Aff Builder → Neg Builder → Aff Closer → Neg Closer → Final POI Round
  • Length:
    • Openers: 2:00 each · No POI allowed · Overtime cut-off: 2:15
    • Builders: 3:00 each · POI window: 0:20–2:40 · One POI allowed per Builder speech · Overtime cut-off: 3:15
    • Closers: 3:00 each · No POI allowed · Overtime cut-off: 3:15
    • Final POI Round: 5:00 total · Full POI rules apply
  • Signals for Openers: 2 bells at 2:00 (stop) · Interrupt at 2:15
  • Signals for Builders: 1 bell at 0:20 (POI window opens) · 1 bell at 2:40 (POI window closes) · 2 bells at 3:00 (stop) · Interrupt at 3:15
  • Signals for Closers: 2 bells at 3:00 (stop) · Interrupt at 3:15

Start timing on the speaker's first word, not when they stand up.

Speech Transition Cues

  1. "The Affirmative Opener will now begin. You have two minutes."
  2. "Thank you. The Negative Opener will now present their case. You have two minutes."
  3. "Thank you. We move to the Affirmative Builder. You have three minutes. The POI window opens at 20 seconds and closes at 2:40. One POI is allowed."
  4. "Thank you. The Negative Builder will now speak. You have three minutes. The POI window opens at 20 seconds and closes at 2:40. One POI is allowed."
  5. "Thank you. The Affirmative Closer will now summarize. You have three minutes. No POIs are allowed during Closer speeches."
  6. "Thank you. The Negative Closer will conclude the six-speech stage. You have three minutes. No POIs are allowed."

Final POI Round Introduction

"Thank you to both teams for the six speeches. We now move to the final Point of Information round, which will last five minutes total. During this stage, teams will alternate asking questions to challenge the opposing side's arguments.

POI rules apply: each question must be brief—10 seconds maximum. The responding team should answer concisely and may accept or decline any POI. Both teams should engage actively but respectfully.

The first POI will come from the affirmative team. Teams, you may begin when ready."

Monitor the clock and intervene at 5:00 with two bells. Allow brief wrap-up if mid-exchange.

Closing Script

"Thank you to both teams. Judges, please finalize your scores and prepare to announce your decision. This debate is now adjourned."

Conduct & Troubleshooting

Conduct Interventions (If Needed)

  • Interruption without POI: "Please hold your comments until your next speech."
  • Personal attacks: "Please address the argument, not the person."
  • Excessive overtime: "Thank you, speaker. We must move to the next speech."
  • POI outside window: Quietly signal "not allowed" with hand gesture.

Maintain a neutral, professional tone. Your role is to facilitate, not to judge content.

Common Situations

  • Speaker hesitates to start: Wait 5 seconds, then say "You may begin when ready."
  • Timer fails: Announce "We will restart this speech" and reset.
  • Judge has a question mid-round: "Judges, please hold questions until after all speeches conclude."
  • Team requests clarification: Briefly restate the motion or format rule, then proceed.

Debate Topic Introductions

Read the appropriate introduction aloud before stating the motion. This provides essential context and frames the ethical stakes.

1. Survival Cannibalism

Introduction: "In rare disasters, people have survived by consuming those who had already died. This raises a difficult choice: is keeping someone alive more important than protecting the dignity of the dead and the peace of the grave? The law against desecrating the dead reflects a societal value that human remains are not mere things. Swedish law protects human remains under gravfridsbrott, while the necessity defense allows exemptions when emergency action prevents serious harm."

Motion: Should Swedish law excuse consumption of human remains in extreme survival situations where no killing occurs?

2. Death Penalty

Introduction: "While Sweden has abolished the death penalty, this debate re-examines the core question. It pits the demand for absolute justice against the principle that a state should not have the authority to kill. Proponents argue it is the only proportionate response to heinous crimes. Opponents argue it is an unacceptable violation of human rights that carries the unforgivable risk of executing an innocent person, even with modern forensics."

Motion: Should the state have the power to impose the death penalty for the most serious crimes?

3. Gene Editing

Introduction: "Gene-editing tools like CRISPR give us the power to correct errors in our DNA that cause devastating inherited diseases like Huntington's or cystic fibrosis. This raises a profound ethical question: should we use this power on human embryos to prevent such conditions before a child is even born? Supporters argue it is a moral duty to prevent suffering when we can. Opponents warn that we are crossing a dangerous line by making permanent changes to the human gene pool for generations who cannot consent."

Motion: Should Sweden allow parents to use gene editing to prevent serious diseases in embryos?

4. Healthcare Rationing

Introduction: "During severe medical crises, hospitals may face impossible choices about who receives life-saving treatment when resources are scarce. Age-based prioritization raises fundamental questions about the value of life and fairness in healthcare allocation. Some argue younger patients have more life-years to gain, while others contend that age-based criteria violate the principle of equal dignity."

Motion: Should hospitals prioritize younger patients over older patients for life-saving treatment during a severe shortage?

5. Organ Donation

Introduction: "Thousands die each year waiting for organ transplants. An opt-out system would presume consent for organ donation unless individuals actively refuse, potentially saving many lives. However, this shifts the default from individual autonomy to state presumption. The question becomes whether the urgent need for organs justifies removing the requirement for explicit consent and family input."

Motion: Should all adults be treated as organ donors at death unless they have opted out, with no family veto?

6. Abortion

Introduction: "The abortion debate centers on a fundamental conflict: when does the state's interest in protecting potential life override an individual's right to bodily autonomy? Supporters of abortion rights argue that forcing someone to continue an unwanted pregnancy violates their fundamental freedoms. Opponents argue that a fetus has moral status that the law must protect, even before birth."

Motion: Should the law prioritize a person's right to choose over the potential life of a fetus?

7. Citizenship Tests

Introduction: "Many countries require citizenship tests covering language, history, and civic values. Supporters argue these ensure integration and shared national identity. Critics contend such tests create unfair barriers, may not accurately measure commitment to a country, and risk excluding worthy candidates based on test performance rather than actual contribution to society."

Motion: Should Sweden add mandatory Swedish and civics tests and a pledge of allegiance as conditions for naturalization?
